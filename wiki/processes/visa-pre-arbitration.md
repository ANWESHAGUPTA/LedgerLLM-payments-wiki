# Visa Pre-Arbitration

## Summary
Pre-arbitration is the stage in Visa's dispute process where an issuer rejects a merchant's representment and escalates the case. It occurs when the issuer finds the merchant's evidence insufficient or incorrect.

## Details
Pre-arbitration ("pre-arbs") happens when issuers reject merchant representment submissions. Common reasons include:

- Missing evidence in the representment
- Evidence provided doesn't refute the buyer's claim
- Evidence doesn't meet reason code requirements
- Missing rebuttal letter or required documents
- Deliberately falsified evidence

### Allocation Workflow Pre-Arbs
For disputes in the Allocation workflow:
- Acquirers and merchants have limited ability to challenge
- 30-day timeframe to respond (acquirers typically set tighter deadlines)
- Combines traditional representment and pre-arbitration phases

### Collaboration Workflow Pre-Arbs
For Consumer Dispute and Processing Error chargebacks:
- Process similar to Mastercard's approach
- Issuer rejects representment and submits pre-arb attempt
- Acquirer may communicate with merchant or act independently
- Can submit pre-arb response or concede the dispute

## Compliance notes
Pre-arbitration is not the same as a "pre-arbitration chargeback" - Visa specifies that pre-arbitration is the correct terminology. Merchants have limited time to respond, typically 30 days or less depending on acquirer requirements.

## Technical reference
Pre-arbs follow different workflows based on dispute category:
- Allocation workflow: fraud and authorization disputes
- Collaboration workflow: consumer disputes and processing errors

## Cross-network comparison
Mastercard uses a similar pre-arbitration process, particularly for consumer disputes and processing errors. Visa's Allocation workflow is more restrictive than Mastercard's equivalent process.
